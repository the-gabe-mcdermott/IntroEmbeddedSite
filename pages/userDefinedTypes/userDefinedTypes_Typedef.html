Type Definitions



A typedef lets you create an alternate name for an existing datatype. Consider these example. 


	//Defines the word "byte"
	//as an unsigned 8 bit integer
	typedef uint8_t byte_t;

	//Defines the word "word"
	//as an unsigned 32 bit integer
	typedef uint32_t word_t;
	
	//Defines a new datatype just
	//for masks.
	typedef uint8_t mask8_t;

It is possible to create multiple typedefs for the same "base" type. That is, you can create multiple typedefs that are uint8_t.

typedef V. #Defines

Typedefs are similar to "#define" statements, which are a form of pre-processor directive that I will explain later in the chapter. The difference is that typdefs are interpreted by the compiler, while #defines are interpreted by the pre-processor.

Put another way, when you mash the button to compile your code, first, the #defines are "swapped in" by the pre-processor. The pre-processor is dumber than the compiler and won't do any error checking or second guessing its job, it will copy and paste all the #defines it finds. After the pre-processor, the compiler is left to interpret the typedefs.
